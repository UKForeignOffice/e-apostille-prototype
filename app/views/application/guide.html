Legalisation and ‘Apostille’ certificates

When you need to share an official UK document with an authority in another country they might ask that the document is ‘legalised’. This is because an authority like an employer or bank needs to be sure your document is genuine.

Official UK documents include birth certificates, passports, UK qualifications, contracts and many other documents. There is no definitive list.

How documents are legalised

Only the Legalisation Office, part of the Foreign, Commonwealth & Development Office (FCDO), can legalise an official UK document.

For a UK document to be legalised it must be:

  • certified/signed by a UK public official to show the document is genuine (they may also add their stamp or seal)
  • submitted to the UK Legalisation Office who will check the document has been correctly signed and dated (they will also check any stamp or seal)
  • legalised through the attachment of a certificate called an ‘Apostille’

The attached Apostille certificate is what makes the document a legalised version. You can then share your legalised document, including its Apostille, with the authority that requested it.

UK public officials

A public official is a professional who is on an official UK register. Examples include notaries, solicitors and GPs.

Notaries are public officials who specialise in the authentication of documents and signatures. Be aware that some overseas authorities might insist that your documents are signed specifically by a notary, rather than a solicitor, for example.

Using a notary or solicitor

Some people use a legal service provider, such as a notary or solicitor, when applying to legalise documents. They may take you through the process and apply for you.

Fees and courier costs

You will pay a fee for each Apostille certificate that is issued.

You can apply for a single Apostille certificate to cover multiple documents. However, if you do so you should check that the authority will accept this. Also be aware that all of the documents will always need to be shared together with the Apostille certificate.

Refunds

If a document cannot be legalised the fee for that document (or batch of documents where a single Apostille was requested) will be automatically refunded.

The Legalisation Office cannot issue a refund if an overseas authority refuses to accept a legalised document. Check what the authority will accept before you apply.

Courier costs

If you use the paper-based service you will also need to pay for courier or postage.

If you do not want to pay for a courier

Your documents will be returned by courier but if you are in the UK you can have them returned by post. You must include an A4-sized envelope with the return address written on it. Attach enough UK postage stamps to cover the return postage.

Before you apply

It is up to you to check:

  • what the overseas authority expects and will accept from you
  • the rules of the UK Legalisation Office for your type of document

Check the authority’s requirements

Check what the authority needs before you apply. For example, some might accept a document ‘certified’ by a notary or solicitor without legalisation.

If the authority needs a legalised version (Apostille), check exactly what it will accept. For example:

  • legalised copy, original document, or either
  • electronic apostille, paper apostille, or either

If legalising more than one document, also check if the authority will accept a single Apostille certificate or requires a separate one for each document.

Check the rules for your document

There are different rules for different types of documents. For example the Legalisation Office is only able to legalise original versions of some types of document and certified copies of other documents.

Standard (paper-based) applications

Use the document checker to select your document type and find out what formats are acceptable.

Electronic (e-Apostille) applications

To use the e-Apostille service your documents must be electronically signed by a UK notary or solicitor using either an:

  • advanced electronic signature (AES)
  • qualified electronic signature (QES)

A simple electronic signature (SES) cannot be used.

Most documents are suitable for the digital e-Apostille service, however the following are excluded:

  • All General Register Office (GRO) documents, including birth, death, marriage, civil partnership and adoption certificates
  • ACRO police certificates for England and Wales
  • Disclosure Barring Service (DBS) certificates for England and Wales
  • Disclosure certificates for Scotland or Northern Ireland
  • Fingerprint certificates
  • Association of Chartered Certified Accountants (ACCA) membership certificates

Prepare your documents

Before a document can be submitted for legalisation it, or in some cases a copy, must first have been signed by a UK public official.

Originals and certified copies

Some documents will have been signed by an official when originally issued. Others, as well as certified copies, will need to be signed separately (often by a notary or solicitor) before you can apply.

There are general rules about certifying a document. However, make sure you also follow any specific rules of the Legalisation Office for your document type.

Apply for legalisation

To make an application you will need:

  • the documents you want to legalise (paper or PDF) in a format that meets the rules for the document types
  • a debit or credit card to pay fees

If applying for a paper apostille you will also need the address you want your legalised documents to be sent to. For example you might want them returned to your address or sent directly to the overseas authority.

If applying for a digital apostille your legalised documents will be returned to you by email.

  1. Step 1 Make your legalisation application

    If your document meets the rules you can apply online for it to be legalised

  2. Step 2 Upload or post your document to the Legalisation Office

    No postage required where the e-Apostille service is used.

  3. Step 3 Pay application fees by debit or credit card

    You will also pay courier or postage costs where paper service is used.

  4. Step 4 Application processing

    The Legalisation Office will check your documents, including signatures, and any stamps or seals. They will legalise the document by attaching an Apostille certificate.

  5. Step 5 Receive your legalised document

    Your documents will be sent by courier or post if you use the paper service.

    If you apply for an e-Apostille you will be emailed as soon as your documents are ready to download.

Share your legalised document

Your legalised document and its Apostille certificate must always be shared together.

Paper Apostilles must be shared physically in paper form (for example, in person or by post). This is so the authority can view the original ink signatures.

Electronic Apostilles (e-Apostilles) must be shared digitally (for example, by email, portal upload, on a memory stick, or in person by showing the Apostille on screen). This is so the authority can check the unique electronic signatures.

With an e-Apostille you can download and digitally share as many copies of your legalised document and its e-Apostille as you want. This means you can share with multiple authorities simultaneously rather than needing to apply for separate paper-based copies for each one.

Find a UK notary or solicitor

Depending on the requirements for your documents, you may need to get them signed by a notary. In some cases you may be able to use a solicitor instead but you should check that the overseas authority will accept this.

You can find a notary or solicitor using official lists:

  • notaries in England and Wales
  • solicitors in England and Wales
  • notaries and solicitors in Scotland
  • notaries and solicitors in Northern Ireland

British consulate notary services

In some countries and circumstances a British consulate may also provide notary services.
